With the relatively unbelievably nice weather we've been having this December, I wanted to get out to place some caches. I didn't have a good name picked out for some of them, so this one celebrates the new year!

New Year's Day was first celebrated in 45 B.C. when the Julian calendar was put in place. Julius Caesar recognized that the Roman calendar, which followed the lunar cycle, needed to be changed. He and astronomer Sosigenes designed the Julian calendar, which follows the solar year. The Julian calendar is based on a 365.25-day-long solar year; but the actual solar (tropical) year is 365.242199 days long, making the Julian year 11 minutes too long. Enter the Gregorian calendar!

The Gregorian calendar is the most widely used civil calendar in the world today. It was introduced in 1582 by Pope Gregory XIII as a slight adjustment to the Julian calendar. While the Julian calendar adds one day every four years, the Gregorian calendar omits the leap day on years evenly divisible by 100, unless the year in question is also evenly divisible by 400.

Celebrating the New Year fell out of practice during the Middle Ages, but again became tradition after the Gregorian calendar became standard. Here in the United States, we like to celebrate by watching the ball drop in Times Square and partying hard!

You're looking for a regular sized container. The terrain could be muddy if the weather has been wet. Have fun!
