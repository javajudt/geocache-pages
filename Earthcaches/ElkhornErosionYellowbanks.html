Geography of the River----------------------------

The Elkhorn River spans 290 miles, flowing from Stuart, NE until it joins the Platte just southwest of Omaha, NE. Its basin area is 7,000 square miles. Tributaries of the Elkhorn River include its own North and South forks, Logan Creek, Rock Creek, and Maple Creek.

The Elkhorn flows east southeast from the source (the elevation here is 2,162 ft), turning more southeast at Inman. The river curves northeast just after Norfolk, back southeast at Pilger, and practically south at West Point until it dumps into the Platte River (with an elevation of 1,070 ft). The waters of the Elkhorn eventually make it to the Gulf of Mexico. The Platte dumps into the Missouri, and the Missouri flows into the Mississippi which flows into the Gulf.

 

What is Erosion?----------------------------

Erosion is a natural process that wears away the land. Because of millions of years of erosion, today we have Chimney Rock, the Grand Canyon, and similar natural wonders.

 

Types of Erosion----------------------------

There are three (3) main types of erosion:Glacier, Water, and Wind.

Glacier: Also known as ice erosion, glacier erosion can be thought of as sandpaper smoothing a piece of wood. Glaciers can level once-rugged terrain, moving boulders as large as a house. During the ice age millions of years ago, glaciers moved down from Santa's Workshop (the North Pole) to as far south as the Ohio and Missouri Rivers, depositing moraines and carving valleys in its path.

Water: Water in a river erodes the riverbanks while water in the ocean erodes the shoreline. Chemicals in the water can even come into play and eat away at the adjacent land. As the water obtains more sediment, erosion becomes more prominent as the sediment knocks into land.

Wind:This type of erosion may seem simple, but it can do big things in open areas. In deserts, for instance, large sand dunes can be created from the wind blowing sand to other areas.

 

Effects of Erosion----------------------------

Land becomes infertile when topsoil is washed away. In a wide open farm, the farmer dreads heavy winds because it will blow away all the good, fertile soil.

When sediment dissolves in water, the water quality is degraded: not as much light can penetrate through the water, nests can be buried in the new sediment, and fishes' gills can become clogged.

Glaciers, as mentioned earlier, have the power to carve valleys on their journey and to deposit moraines at the end of their trek.

Water rushing against riverbanks can grind down the bank to a flat wall--or a large canyon in the Grand Canyon's case! Sea water crashing on the seashore is able to reform the beach.
